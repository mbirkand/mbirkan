Unit 1 - Ethics and AI Ethics: Key Concepts and Career Paths

Ethics and How Ethics Work in AI

Ethics refers to principles that guide human behavior, determining what is right or wrong. In AI, ethics involves ensuring that AI technologies are developed and deployed in ways that align with fairness, transparency, and accountability (Deckard, 2023). This involves addressing issues such as privacy, bias, and the potential for harm.

Correa et al. (2023) Overview

According to Correa et al. (2023), the field of AI ethics has seen significant growth, with numerous guidelines being created by governments, organizations, and academic institutions worldwide. However, there is a challenge in reaching a global consensus on the values that should guide AI development, given the diverse perspectives of different stakeholders. Their analysis of 200 governance policies highlights common principles like transparency, accountability, and fairness, but also notes significant regional differences and the need for better tools to compare these policies globally.

Career Path in AI Ethics

Deckard (2023) emphasizes that a career in AI ethics requires a multidisciplinary approach, combining technical expertise with an understanding of ethical principles. Aspiring AI ethicists should focus on building a foundation in both technology and ethics, stay informed on AI developments, and engage in public policy discussions. Collaborating with professionals from various fields and developing practical solutions to real-world problems are essential skills for success in this area.

Recommended Actions and Impact

Given the rapidly evolving landscape of AI ethics, professionals should advocate for global standards that balance both short-term risks (like privacy breaches and bias) and long-term challenges (such as AI autonomy). Participating in shaping public policies and fostering interdisciplinary collaborations can help address the ethical concerns raised by AI. This proactive approach will ensure that AI technologies serve the public interest while mitigating potential legal, social, and professional challenges. In conclusion, the growing demand for AI ethics highlights the importance of developing a career that integrates ethical thinking with technical expertise. With ongoing efforts to create governance frameworks, aspiring AI ethicists can play a vital role in shaping the future of AI in a way that aligns with global ethical standards

Unit 3

In Unit 3, I learned about key aspects of research design and the importance of selecting the right approach based on the research objective. I was introduced to two main types of research design:

  • Exploratory Research, which is used to explore specific aspects of a research area when the problem is not clearly defined. This type of research helps gain a better understanding of a situation or issue, though it does not provide conclusive results. An example of exploratory research is investigating the effectiveness of Customer Relationship Management (CRM) in mobile marketing.
  • Conclusive Research, which focuses on verifying insights and supporting decision-making. A key category within this type is Descriptive Research, where the goal is to describe causes or factors in a specific area. For example, analyzing the effectiveness of social media as a marketing tool is a form of descriptive research.

I also gained an understanding of how interviews and surveys can be integrated into research to gather data, and I learned how pre- and post-testing can be applied to measure changes or impacts in a study. This unit provided a strong foundation for understanding different research methods and how to choose the right one for my investigation.

Unit 4

In Unit 4, I was introduced to various data collection methods commonly used in qualitative research, though they can also be applied in quantitative research depending on the study's needs. By the end of this unit, I gained the ability to understand how to effectively use these methods and determine which would be suitable for my investigation based on the type of data needed.

The key methods covered include:

  • Case Studies: These involve detailed research of individuals or groups to develop hypotheses and broaden the scope for studying a phenomenon. While useful for generating insights, case studies are not ideal for determining cause and effect due to potential biases and the risk of weak generalizations if atypical respondents are involved.
  • Focus Groups: This method involves gathering a group of 6-10 people with common backgrounds to discuss and provide insights on a specific topic. Focus groups are valuable for answering "why," "what," and "how" questions, but careful selection of participants is crucial to ensure the data is relevant and reliable.
  • Quantitative Observation: In this method, numerical data such as weight, volume, or other measurable values are collected from a sample. This objective data is then analyzed statistically to derive conclusions. Surveys and experiments are commonly used in this approach.
  • Qualitative Observation: This involves observing characteristics and behaviors from a distance, allowing the researcher to monitor respondents in their natural environment. It provides deeper insights, such as tracking customer purchasing habits in a supermarket. The researcher can choose different levels of involvement, from being a passive observer to an active participant.

This unit helped me understand how to apply these methods and decide which would best fit the goals of my research, considering the type of data I aim to collect.

Unit 5

The Cambridge Analytica scandal (2018) is one of the most infamous examples of the misuse of data collected through innocuous surveys. Cambridge Analytica obtained data from millions of Facebook users via a personality quiz app, exploiting Facebook's API, which allowed access to not only the quiz-takers’ data but also their friends' data. This information was used for political profiling and targeted advertising, influencing elections in the UK and USA without users' explicit consent (Confessore, 2018). The incident raised major ethical and legal concerns regarding data privacy and manipulation of personal information for political gains.

Further Examples of Inappropriate Survey Usage

  • Google Location Tracking (2018): Google was found to collect users' location data even when users had disabled location tracking. This was done through apps and surveys embedded in Google services, raising concerns about transparency and consent. The data was primarily used for advertising, revealing an abuse of user trust (Fox, 2018).
  • Equifax Data Breach (2017): Although not survey-based, Equifax's breach exposed the data of over 147 million individuals due to inadequate security protocols. This case highlighted the risks of storing large volumes of personal data without proper protection, which was later used for identity theft and fraud (Krebs, 2017).
Ethical, Social, Legal, and Professional Implications

These examples demonstrate clear ethical violations related to privacy and transparency. Users were not fully informed of how their data would be used, breaching consent and autonomy principles. The social impact is significant, eroding public trust in technology platforms, while contributing to political manipulation and data misuse. Legally, these cases resulted in fines and stricter data protection laws like the GDPR, emphasizing the need for regulatory oversight. From a professional standpoint, these incidents underscore the responsibility of computing professionals to prioritize user privacy, adhere to ethical data handling practices, and uphold public trust.

Unit 6

In this unit, I was introduced to quantitative methods, which focus on examining the relationships between variables that are measured numerically and analyzed through statistical and graphical techniques. Quantitative research is often used to test hypotheses, measure outcomes, and make predictions, and it involves organizing data into datasets where variables are analyzed through various methods.

A key focus of the unit was on descriptive statistics, which help summarize and make sense of data in a meaningful way. This includes graphical summaries to visually represent data, and two important summary measures: measures of location and measures of dispersion. Measures of location, such as the mean or median, help to identify a typical observation in the dataset, while measures of dispersion, like range or standard deviation, provide insight into the variability or spread of the data.

Additionally, I learned about the different levels of measurement for variables, which is essential for choosing the correct statistical methods. By understanding these concepts, I now have the ability to apply descriptive statistics, identify levels of measurement, and calculate measures of location and spread for a given dataset, helping to extract valuable insights from quantitative data.

Exercise 7.2

In order to determine whether the mean income for males exceeds that of females, a two-sample t-test assuming equal variances was performed using Excel’s Data Analysis Toolpack. The dataset contains income data for 60 male and 60 female cardholders of a bank’s "Superplus Diamond" card. The t-test compares the mean income of Variable 1 (males) and Variable 2 (females) to see if there is a significant difference.

The results show that the mean income for males is £52.91k, while the mean income for females is £44.23k. The t-statistic is 3.268, and the one-tailed p-value is 0.00071, which is well below the significance level of 0.05. This means we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the mean income for males is significantly higher than that for females. The assumptions of the t-test include normality of the data and equal variances, which were addressed by using the two-sample test assuming equal variances based on the comparable variances of the two groups.

Unit 8

In Unit 8, I learned about the process of hypothesis testing and its key components. The unit emphasized starting with a clear null hypothesis (H0), which is a statement about a population parameter that we assume to be true unless proven otherwise. The alternative hypothesis (H1) contradicts the null hypothesis and is tested through statistical analysis.

I learned how to set the criteria for decision-making, typically using a significance level (commonly 5%), which determines the threshold for rejecting or accepting the null hypothesis. The test statistic is calculated to compare the sample data against the null hypothesis, allowing us to make a decision. If the probability (p-value) is lower than the significance level, we reject the null hypothesis; otherwise, we accept it.

The unit also covered the importance of sampling distributions and the role of the p-value in determining whether to reject or accept the null hypothesis. I explored the concept of Type 1 and Type 2 errors, where Type 1 is incorrectly rejecting a true null hypothesis, and Type 2 is failing to reject a false one. The unit also discussed the differences between related and unrelated samples and their impact on the power of hypothesis tests.

Finally, I learned the importance of setting hypotheses before analyzing data to ensure an unbiased and impartial test, and the nuances involved in reporting significance levels accurately. This unit deepened my understanding of inferential statistics and the rigorous process involved in hypothesis testing.
